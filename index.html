The novel was written by Charles Dickens and published in 1837.
The story begins with a woman who takes refuge in a workhouse to give birth, but unfortunately she dies during childbirth and her ring and pendant are stolen by old Sally.

David Copperfield

industrial working class - ⁠gap between social classes - ⁠discrimination in education

David Copperfield is a novel written by Charles Dickens, first published between 1849 and 1850.
It is considered one of the author's masterpieces and one of the most autobiographical works of English literature.
The novel tells the story of David Copperfield, a young orphan who tries to realise his dreams at a time of great social and economic transformation caused by the Industrial Revolution.
One of the main themes of the novel is social inequality between different classes of people, which is also reflected in discrimination in education.
David Copperfield lives his childhood and adolescence in conditions of poverty and exploitation, working as a blacksmith, as a clerk and as a teacher.
He has no access to adequate education and faces many difficulties in obtaining a professional qualification.
Even when he manages to get into Oxford, the prestigious English university, he has to suffer injustice and bullying from his richer and more privileged peers.
Another important theme of the novel is the search for one's identity and destiny.
David Copperfield has to face many changes in his life, including his marriage to Agnes Wickfield, the birth of his children, his relationship with Uriah Heep, his success as a writer and his love for Dora Spenlow.
The novel shows how David is able to overcome adversity and find happiness through his strength of spirit, generosity and faith.
David Copperfield is a very interesting novel to read because it offers a historical and social view of 19th century England, but also a personal and emotional story of a protagonist searching for his place in the world.













Hard Times

treatment of the working class

"Hard Times" is a novel written by Charles Dickens and published in 1854. Set in the industrial town of Coketown, the book critically explores the treatment of the working class during the Victorian era.
In the novel, Dickens highlights the dehumanizing consequences of industrialization on people's lives, emphasizing the lack of empathy and the rigidity of social institutions.
The plot revolves around the Gradgrind family, representing the utilitarian and rationalistic mindset of the time.
The working class is portrayed as victims of oppressive working conditions and a lack of social support. Characters like Stephen Blackpool, a textile worker, are trapped in cycles of poverty and exploitation.
Dickens also criticizes education that emphasizes only useful facts, neglecting the human and creative aspects of life.
Through "Hard Times," Dickens aims to raise awareness about social injustices and promote greater compassion and understanding toward the working class.
The novel is a powerful commentary on the need for social equity in an era of rapid industrial change.

Dickens presents his view of a harsh legal system because his father was imprisoned when he was a boy.
